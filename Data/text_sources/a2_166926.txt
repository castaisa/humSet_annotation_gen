As a result of these interventions, access to education by migrant children has improved since 2018, when the TLC provided the only access,21 and since mid-2019 when 90 per cent of parents with school-age children indicated that their children did not have access to formal education.22 At the end of 2019, over 1,300 children were pursuing their education at CFS (around 600 in those operated by LWC),23 representing around 70 per cent of school-age migrant children. In addition, 771 migrant children had been granted student permits by the Ministry of National Security in 2019, enabling them to be enrolled in state schools.24 While it is not possible to estimate how many of these were actually enrolled,25 it is apparent that a majority of migrant children were attending CFS or other schools prior to the school closures forced by COVID-19 in March 2020
